Maggie had never heard the phrase "Accidental American." She had always considered herself Irish. Born and raised in Ireland, she had never lived in the United States, never voted in a U.S. election, and never thought of herself as American. Then an unexpected conversation with her accountant changed everything.

"You know you're a U.S. citizen, right?" he asked.

Maggie was stunned. "What do you mean? I've never lived in the U.S."

Because her parents were U.S. citizens, she had automatically acquired U.S. citizenship at birth, even though she'd never set foot in America. And with that citizenship came something she'd never considered: tax obligations to the U.S. government.

## What is an Accidental American?

An Accidental American is someone considered a U.S. citizen under U.S. law but with little to no connection to the country. That often includes people who were born abroad to U.S. citizen parents, were born in the U.S. but moved away as children, or hold dual citizenship without realizing the U.S. still considers them liable for taxes.

Maggie fit the first category. Although she'd never lived in the U.S., she was still legally a citizen and subject to citizenship-based taxation, one of the biggest challenges accidental Americans face.

## The tax challenges

The United States is one of the only countries that taxes its citizens no matter where they live. Even though Maggie had lived her whole life in Ireland and paid taxes there, the U.S. still required her to file a U.S. tax return every year (even if she owed nothing), report her worldwide income, disclose foreign bank accounts above a threshold under FBAR rules, and comply with FATCA, which requires foreign banks to report accounts held by U.S. citizens.

The consequences for non-compliance can be serious. The IRS can impose heavy penalties even without intent, and her Irish bank had already flagged her as a U.S. citizen under FATCA, which can mean banking restrictions until her tax situation is sorted out.

## What can Accidental Americans do?

**Determine your filing obligations.** You may need Form 1040, an FBAR if your non-U.S. accounts exceed $10,000 at any point in the year, and Form 8938 if your foreign financial assets exceed the FATCA threshold.

**Use the Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures.** This IRS program lets people who weren't aware of their obligations catch up on filings, avoid severe penalties, and get back into compliance without unnecessary stress.

**Consider renouncing U.S. citizenship, if it makes sense for you.** This isn't a decision to take lightly. It involves filing several years of back taxes, paying a renunciation fee, and potentially owing an exit tax if your assets exceed a certain threshold.
