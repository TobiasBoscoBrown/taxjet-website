If you're a professional who relocated to the U.S. to work and live, you may still hold foreign assets back home. At tax time, you may wonder whether you have to mention them on your U.S. return. Here's what you need to know.

## The IRS rule

Under the Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA), U.S. taxpayers with foreign financial assets above certain thresholds must report them on Form 8938. The IRS publishes the details and current thresholds on its website.

## The process

We help you declare foreign assets when their value exceeds the threshold, including foreign bank accounts, investment accounts, and even life insurance. A simplified roadmap:

1. **Identify your assets.** Make a list of all your foreign holdings.
2. **Determine the value.** Calculate the total value of those assets.
3. **Fill out the forms.** Primarily Form 8938 with your return, plus the FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) if your foreign accounts exceed $10,000 at any point in the year.
4. **Submit on time.** File by the deadline, usually April 15th.

## Consequences of not reporting

Skipping your reporting duties can lead to real problems. Monetary penalties can reach $10,000 per undisclosed asset, and willful failures can rise to $100,000 or 50% of the asset's value, whichever is greater. Unreported assets can also trigger additional taxes and interest, and non-disclosure may invite a broader audit.

## How likely is the IRS to find out?

Quite likely. The IRS has strong partnerships with tax authorities worldwide, and FATCA requires foreign financial institutions to report information about U.S. account holders. It can take time, but undisclosed assets tend to surface.

## What to do if you didn't report

Don't panic. The IRS offers a Voluntary Disclosure Program that lets taxpayers come forward and report previously undisclosed assets; penalties are often less severe than if the IRS finds them first. Consult a tax professional to guide the disclosure and minimize penalties, and stay informed about your obligations going forward.

Foreign asset reporting can seem daunting, but it's a critical part of your financial responsibilities in the U.S. Stay proactive, informed, and compliant.
