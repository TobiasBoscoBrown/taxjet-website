If you're an American living abroad, you may have heard of the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE), a tax benefit that lets U.S. expats exclude a portion of their foreign-earned income from U.S. taxes. But did you know there's an additional break called the Foreign Housing Exclusion (FHE) that can reduce your bill even further?

If you've never heard of it, don't worry. This guide explains what it is, how it works, who qualifies, and how to claim it.

## What is the Foreign Housing Exclusion?

The Foreign Housing Exclusion lets you exclude certain housing expenses from your taxable income if you live abroad and qualify for the FEIE. In plain terms, money you spend on rent, utilities, and other eligible housing costs may lower your U.S. tax bill. Think of it as a way to offset the high cost of living abroad, especially in expensive cities like London, Tokyo, or Singapore.

## How does it work?

It comes down to three steps. First, qualify for the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion. Second, calculate your eligible housing expenses (some, like rent, count; others, like buying a home, do not). Third, subtract the base housing amount the IRS assumes you'd spend anyway.

## Who qualifies?

To claim the Foreign Housing Exclusion you must first qualify for the FEIE by meeting one of two tests:

- **Bona Fide Residence Test:** you live in a foreign country full-time and have established residency there, staying at least one full tax year (January 1 to December 31).
- **Physical Presence Test:** you spend at least 330 full days in a foreign country within a 12-month period. The days don't have to be consecutive, but they must total 330.

## What housing expenses are covered?

Eligible costs include rent, utilities (except TV, internet, or phone), property insurance, furniture rental, parking required for housing, and repairs needed to live in the home.

Not covered: buying a home (mortgage or down payment), home improvements, and luxuries like maid service or a swimming pool.

## How much can you exclude?

There's a formula. Take your total eligible housing expenses, then subtract the base housing amount (set at 16% of the FEIE limit, about $18,000 in 2024). The remainder is your exclusion.

For example, if your rent and utilities total $40,000 and the base amount is $18,000, you could exclude $22,000 from your taxable income.

Some expensive cities have higher limits. Recent caps include Tokyo around $61,800, London around $47,600, and Dubai around $46,200. The IRS updates these each year.

## How to claim it

Fill out IRS Form 2555 (the same form used for the FEIE), enter your housing expenses in the right section, and submit it with your Form 1040.

A few things to keep in mind: you must have foreign earned income, it's an exclusion rather than a deduction, the self-employed still owe self-employment tax, and you can't double-dip by also deducting the same expenses under the Foreign Tax Credit.
