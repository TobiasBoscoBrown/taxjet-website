As a U.S. citizen living abroad, life can be exciting and full of new experiences, whether you're working in another country, traveling, or soaking up the local culture. But one thing might be flying under the radar: your U.S. tax obligations, and specifically how not filing could affect your future Social Security benefits.

The good news is you won't lose your Social Security benefits just for not filing. But not filing can complicate things later. Here's how taxes and Social Security connect, and why filing still matters.

## Earning Social Security credits as an expat

To qualify for benefits (retirement, disability, or survivor), you need to earn Social Security credits, typically by working and paying Social Security taxes in the U.S. But what if you're abroad and not earning U.S. income?

You can still qualify. Many countries have a Totalization Agreement with the U.S. that lets you combine work credits earned abroad with credits earned in the U.S. So even if you're no longer paying into U.S. Social Security, you may still meet the threshold. And once you're eligible (usually 40 credits, about 10 years of work), you don't need to keep earning credits to keep your benefits.

## Why filing still matters

Not filing doesn't automatically cost you benefits, but it can cause problems. When you eventually apply, you'll need to prove your work history, and the IRS records help document it. If you've never filed or kept up with returns, it can be harder to prove eligibility. Filing also helps the IRS track your foreign income so you're properly credited.

## Keeping benefits without U.S. income

As long as you've earned the required credits, you keep your benefits even without U.S.-based income. You can also collect your Social Security while living abroad, anywhere in the world. Just remember to report any foreign income correctly to avoid issues with the IRS or your future eligibility.

## Key takeaways

Not filing doesn't mean you lose your benefits, but it can complicate proving your work history later. You don't need U.S.-based income to receive benefits once you've earned enough credits. Expats may still qualify under Totalization Agreements. And filing keeps your records accurate, which makes claiming benefits smoother when the time comes.
