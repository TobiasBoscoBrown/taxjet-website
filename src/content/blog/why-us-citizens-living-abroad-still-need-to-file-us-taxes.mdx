First off, congratulations on this exciting chapter of your life. Whether you're working abroad, traveling long-term, engaged to someone from another country, or just soaking up the sun, life as an expat comes with endless adventure. But here's something you might not realize while living your best life overseas: you could still need to file your U.S. taxes.

Yes, even thousands of miles away, your tax obligations don't go on vacation. Understanding this early can save you headaches down the road, and it can actually save you money too.

## Why you could still need to file

The U.S. is one of the only countries that taxes its citizens based on citizenship, not residency. Whether you're in Paris, Tokyo, or a beachside bungalow in Mexico, the IRS expects a U.S. return every year, even if you have no intention of returning. The good news: you don't have to pay double taxes. Benefits like the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) and the Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) can significantly reduce your liability.

## Understanding the Foreign Tax Credit

The FTC helps you avoid double taxation. If you pay income taxes to the country where you live and work, you can claim those taxes as a credit against your U.S. taxes. In some cases that credit can reduce your U.S. liability to zero, or even result in a refund.

## Could you get a refund? Yes.

There are a few ways it can happen. If the foreign taxes you paid are greater than the U.S. tax you owe, the IRS lets you carry the excess credit forward or back. If you qualify for the FEIE and have already had U.S. tax withheld, you could get a refund when you file. And if you've had U.S. taxes withheld or paid estimates while your actual liability is lower thanks to the FTC and FEIE, you could be refunded the excess.

## What happens if you don't file?

It's easy to think the IRS won't find out, but it has increasingly sophisticated ways of tracking income worldwide. Failing to report foreign accounts (the FBAR) can carry penalties of $10,000 per violation. Not filing can also complicate future Social Security benefits and any decision to renounce citizenship later.

On the statute of limitations: the IRS generally has three years to audit a return, but that clock doesn't start if you never filed. Missing filings means the IRS can go back indefinitely.

## Programs that help you catch up

If you've missed filings, the IRS offers paths back. The Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures are for those who weren't willfully ignoring their responsibilities. The Delinquent International Information Return Submission Procedures help those who missed forms like the FBAR without intending to evade taxes.
